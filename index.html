01
Question One

Website Audit, Messaging & Competitive Analysis

First impression. Evatt AI is an AI assistant for legal professionals - covering research, drafting, and document review. The ICP appears to be solo lawyers and small-to-mid-sized firms who need AI tooling without enterprise procurement.

The product is clear enough. The reason to trust it is not.

Within 30 seconds, a prospective user - especially a lawyer - will ask: who else is using this? What firm? What outcome? The website doesn't answer that. Features are listed. Trust is not established.

"What would stop me from signing up: no social proof above the fold, no case studies, no firm logos, no visible founder presence. For legal professionals, trust is the product."
Missing
Trust Signals
No customer logos, no testimonials, no case studies, no usage numbers, no press mentions. Every element lawyers look for before trusting a new tool.
Missing
Content Infrastructure
No comparison page (Evatt vs Harvey vs Clio vs ChatGPT), no ROI calculator, no use-case pages by practice area.
Gap
Messaging
The website explains what Evatt does. It doesn't explain why Evatt is the right choice over the three other tools a lawyer already has open in another tab.
Gap
Founder Presence
None visible. For an early-stage B2B product in a trust-sensitive category, this is a meaningful gap. Ashley's story is an asset that isn't being used.

That's a large, underserved audience with real AI curiosity and no trusted voice. Evatt could own it.

Question Two

First 90 Days

Days 1-30
Listen Before You Build
Week one: no output. Only input. Talk to Ashley - understand what's working in sales, what objections keep coming up, what the product roadmap looks like for the next two quarters.

Weeks two and three: interview 15 to 20 customers. Not a survey. Actual conversations. Why did you sign up, what made you stay, what nearly made you leave, what would you tell another lawyer about this product.

Week four: interview three to five lost prospects. The reasons people didn't buy are more valuable than the reasons they did.

Deliverable by day 30: Clear ICP profile, top three deal-killing objections, one retention-driving feature, one conversion hypothesis.
Days 31-60
Build the Foundation
ICP framework - documented, not assumed. Messaging framework - one version per segment. Customer story engine - one published case study by day 60. Founder content engine - a lightweight editorial process for turning Ashley's ideas into weekly LinkedIn content. Not ghostwriting. A system.
Days 61-90
Launch and Measure
Customer story live on website and in sales decks. Newsletter issue one published. LinkedIn content cadence running (three posts per week, founder-led). Sales enablement kit: one-pager, objection handling guide, competitive comparison sheet.
Success at day 90: demo requests up from baseline. LinkedIn growth measurable. One customer story live. Sales team using PMM-produced assets. Newsletter at 200 subscribers minimum.

Priority: small and mid-sized firms. They have the pain, the budget, and the decision-making speed. They are also the segment most likely to feel underserved - too small for Harvey, too serious for ChatGPT. Large firms are buying Harvey. Not worth pursuing until Evatt has enterprise compliance documentation and a named-firm reference list.

The core positioning is not "AI for lawyers." Every tool says that.

The message is: The AI associate that helps your firm research, draft and review legal work in minutes - without adding headcount.

Messaging Framework

01
Problem
Time is being spent in the wrong place
Lawyers at small and mid-sized firms spend 30 to 40 percent of their time on research and document review that could be done faster.
02
Impact
Fewer billable hours. Slower turnaround.
That time cost translates directly into a competitive disadvantage against larger firms with more resources - and a ceiling on what the firm can take on.
03
Solution
Evatt handles the retrieval. You handle the judgment.
Research, drafting, and contract review in a fraction of the time. Outputs that are legally grounded, source-cited, and ready for attorney review.
04
Proof
Specific. Sourced. Lawyer-verified.
Customer outcome stat. Time saved per week. Accuracy benchmark. Testimonials from practising lawyers, not tech reviewers.

Objection Handling

Objection 1
"What if it gets something wrong?"
Evatt surfaces sources. Every output is reviewable. You stay in control. The lawyer signs off - not the AI.
Objection 2
"ChatGPT already does this."
ChatGPT doesn't know Australian legal citation standards, doesn't stay updated on case law, and wasn't built for legal liability. Evatt was.
Objection 3
"We don't have time to learn a new tool."
Onboarding takes 20 minutes. Most lawyers are productive in their first session. No IT team required.

Question Four

Founder-Led Growth & Personal Branding

What Ashley should own: Not "AI expert." Not "legal tech founder." Those are crowded positions.

Ashley should own: Practical AI for Lawyers. The person who explains what AI actually does in legal practice - and what it doesn't. Credible because she built the product. Trusted because she doesn't oversell.

Platform priority: LinkedIn first. That's where Australian lawyers with buying authority spend professional time. Everything else - X, YouTube, podcasts - is secondary and should only be added once LinkedIn is working.

Pillar 1
Real Use Cases
"Here's what Evatt did with a contract review last week." Specific. Grounded. Not hypothetical. These posts build credibility faster than any thought leadership.
Pillar 2
AI Myth-Busting
"Why ChatGPT is not a legal research tool." Posts that challenge assumptions travel further than posts that confirm them.
Pillar 3
Behind the Product
What we built, why, what we learned. Builds trust with lawyers evaluating whether to trust Ashley's judgment.
Pillar 4
Customer Stories
Practising lawyers in their own words. Not testimonials. Stories with context, challenge, and outcome.

30-Day LinkedIn Strategy

Week 1 - Establish POV
Define the position before earning the audience
Two posts on what AI can and cannot do in legal work. One post introducing Ashley's reason for building Evatt. No product pitching. Just perspective.
Week 2 - Customer Proof
Let the results speak first
One real outcome from a real user. Plus one post on the most common objection Ashley hears from lawyers evaluating the product.
Week 3 - Educational Depth
Teach, don't sell
One deep post on a specific use case (contract review, research, drafting). One post on a legal AI misconception that keeps coming up.
Week 4 - Engage
Pull the audience in
One question post ("What's the task you most wish AI could do for your firm?"). One recap of what the audience has been asking. One announcement or product update.

Example Posts

A
Ashley - Founder, Evatt AI
Making AI practical for Australian lawyers
The way lawyers do legal research is changing. Not because AI is replacing legal judgment. It isn't. Because AI is eliminating the part of research that was never about judgment in the first place - scanning case after case to find the two that actually matter. A lawyer I spoke with last month described it this way: "I used to spend two hours finding the cases. Now I spend two hours actually thinking about them." That shift is the real story. AI doesn't make legal decisions. It handles the retrieval so lawyers can focus on the analysis. The firms that understand this distinction will move faster. The ones that don't will keep spending billable hours on work that doesn't require their expertise.
A
Ashley - Founder, Evatt AI
Making AI practical for Australian lawyers
Most lawyers aren't afraid AI will replace them. They're afraid it'll be wrong. And confidently wrong. In front of a client. Or worse, in a filing. That fear is rational. The early wave of legal AI tools was built by people who understood AI but didn't understand legal practice. The outputs were impressive until they weren't. A few high-profile hallucination incidents in court documents did real damage to the category's credibility. What's changed: the tools built specifically for legal work - trained on legal data, designed for attorney review, with source citations - are meaningfully different from general-purpose AI. The hesitation isn't a problem to overcome with marketing. It's a problem to overcome with evidence. Show the output. Show the source. Show what the lawyer reviewed and what they changed. That's what builds trust in this category.

Question Six

Product Launch - AI Contract Review Scorer

4 Weeks Out - Pre-Launch
Build anticipation, not awareness
Waitlist with a clear value proposition: "Know the risk level of any contract in under 2 minutes." No feature jargon.

Founder teaser content on LinkedIn: three posts over three weeks. Week one - the problem. Week two - a sneak peek without the full reveal. Week three - what beta users are saying.

Beta group: 10 to 15 existing customers. Their feedback shapes the launch narrative. Their quotes become launch assets.
Launch Week
Evidence over announcement
Day 1: Founder LinkedIn post with a real example. Not a product screenshot - a before-and-after on an actual contract.
Day 2: Newsletter issue dedicated to the launch. Educational framing.
Day 3: Webinar. Live demo with a practising lawyer co-presenting. This is the trust signal.
Day 5: Customer story from beta. Specific outcome, specific contract type, specific time saved.
Post-Launch
Adoption is the real launch
In-app onboarding flow. Three-email adoption sequence. Customer success outreach to high-value accounts with a personal note.

Pricing Model

Tiered subscription with a 14-day free trial on the full product. No feature-gating in the trial. Let lawyers experience the real thing.

Tier 1
Solo & Small Firm
Core research, drafting, and review. Monthly subscription, cancel anytime. Self-serve onboarding. Designed to remove friction from the first purchase decision.
Tier 2
Mid-Sized Firm
Everything in Tier 1 plus Contract Review Scorer, multi-user access, and usage reporting. Demo-led sales motion. Higher ACV, longer relationship.
Lawyers are competitive. The message is not "your competitors are beating you." That's cheap.

The message is: "The lawyers moving fastest right now aren't working harder. They're reviewing contracts in a fraction of the time and spending the rest on the work that actually requires them." Aspiration, not fear.

PMM's role in ARR: PMM is not a support function. It's a revenue function. At Evatt's stage, PMM directly affects ARR through conversion rate on demo requests, expansion revenue, and reduced churn. The metric I'd own: demo-to-paid conversion rate.

Question Seven

Team Design & Prioritisation

As Evatt's first PMM, my primary responsibility would be creating clarity before creating scale.

In the first 6–12 months, I would focus on:

  • Customer research
  • ICP refinement
  • Messaging and positioning
  • Founder content systems
  • Product launches
  • Sales enablement
  • Customer proof and case studies

The goal is to establish repeatable messaging and acquisition motions before investing heavily in channel expansion.

Early-stage companies rarely suffer from a lack of channels. They suffer from unclear positioning. My first priority would be ensuring Evatt's messaging is repeatable, differentiated, and proven before scaling distribution.

Recommended Hiring Sequence

Month 6
Content & Marketing Associate
Increase founder content capacity, customer storytelling, and execution velocity. This role helps operationalise the marketing engine without adding unnecessary complexity.
Month 12
Demand Generation Manager
Once positioning and messaging are validated, focus shifts to scaling acquisition through SEO, partnerships, paid campaigns, and webinar programs.
Month 18-24
Customer Marketing Manager
As the customer base grows, retention, advocacy, expansion revenue, onboarding, and customer education become dedicated growth levers.
